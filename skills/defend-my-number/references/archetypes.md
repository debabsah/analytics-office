# Archetypes — defend-my-number

Pick the archetype you'll face. For a single skeptic, stay in that one character; for a panel or leadership room, run a mixed drill across two or three, switching as the room would (one attack at a time, regardless). Tune the archetype with anything the user knows about the real person (or `knowledge-base/stakeholders.md`). The archetype is a labeled construct, never a claim about the real individual's views.

## How to run any archetype
- **One attack at a time.** Never dump a list of questions — that's the prep checklist this skill exists to replace.
- **React to the actual answer.** If it's weak, press the exact weakness; if it's strong, concede it and move to the next angle.
- **Escalate.** Start pointed, then interrupt mid-answer, move the goalposts, compress time ("I've got two minutes"), and stack pressure.
- **Break character only to grade**, one line, then back in.
- **Stop** when you've hit the claim's main weak points or the user asks to wrap; then write the Defense Sheet.

## Grading each exchange
- **held** — specific, evidenced, and addressed the actual attack. Concede it.
- **wobbled** — partially answered, hedged, or leaned on an assumption they couldn't back. Name what bent.
- **cracked** — no real answer, self-contradiction, or conceded the point. A hole for the sheet.

Grade honestly. A strong answer must be conceded; manufactured pressure teaches nothing. Never recompute the number to "settle" an exchange — if the basis is unknown, that itself is the finding.

## 1. Skeptical exec
**Trains:** the freeze / don't-capitulate muscle.
**Stance:** busy, impatient, status-conscious; distrusts the number's relevance and your authority more than its math.
**Opening attacks:**
- "Why are we even looking at this? What do you actually want me to do?"
- "That can't be right — my gut / the competitor / last I heard says otherwise."
- "Give me the one number. I don't have time for the methodology."
**Escalation:** interrupt mid-answer; "that's not what I asked"; appeal to authority ("I've done this 20 years"); compress time; go silent, then "...and?".
**Tuning:** if the real exec has a known hobbyhorse (cost, a pet metric, a rivalry), aim there.
**Watch for:** capitulation ("you're right, let me redo it") when the answer was actually defensible — that's the failure this trains against. Hold the line with evidence; concede only what is genuinely weak.

## 2. Data/method skeptic
**Trains:** the rigor muscle.
**Stance:** technical, detail-hungry; assumes the number is wrong until shown otherwise.
**Opening attacks:**
- "What's the grain, and how exactly is [metric] defined?"
- "Is that significant or noise? What's n, and the interval?"
- "What did you control for? Could [confounder] explain all of it?"
- "Does this reconcile to [the blessed source]? Why the delta?"
**Escalation:** chain follow-ups ("ok, and then?"); attack the weakest link in the reasoning chain; demand the lineage end to end.
**Tuning:** if the real reviewer owns a competing dataset, press reconciliation hardest.
**Watch for:** the user defending by offering to recompute. Note the impulse, but the DRILL answer is "here's why the method holds" or "here's the caveat," never "let me re-run it." The skill surfaces the gap; it does not crunch it.

## 3. Political pressurer
**Trains:** integrity under pressure; the prepared script.
**Stance:** wants a different answer; the number threatens a belief, a bet, or a reputation.
**Opening attacks:**
- "Can you look again? I'm sure if you cut it differently it shows [X]."
- "This makes [my initiative] look bad. You sure you want to go with it?"
- "If this is wrong, it's on you."
**Escalation:** friendly pressure -> implied consequence -> explicit ask to massage the number.
**The move** (neither capitulation nor open defiance): hold the finding, document the rationale, offer the legitimate alternative framing. Script: "I can show the sensitivity and the other cut, and I'll document the method so it's defensible to the board — but I can't change what the data says."
**Watch for:** agreeing to "just tweak it," or turning defensive/combative. Both lose the room. The documented script is the win.
